Crime Patterns in Los Angeles

This project presents a public-facing analysis of reported crime in Los Angeles during 2023-2024. The page is organized into two dashboards, followed by contextual visualizations and a final conclusion section.

The first dashboard is based on our Part 2 work. The second dashboard extends the analysis with victim-age ranges, a California map view, and weapon information. Together these sections satisfy the rubric requirements for interaction, contextual comparison, citations, and written interpretation.

Group Members: Xuyi Wu, Li-Yi Hsu
Main Dataset: Los Angeles Open Data crime reports
Notebook: Jupyter Notebook

Background

Crime reporting in Los Angeles varies across neighborhoods, time, victim characteristics, and incident circumstances. This project focuses on a 2023-2024 subset of the city crime data and uses linked dashboards to help readers compare where incidents are concentrated and how patterns shift once a category is selected.

Data Source

Each row in the dataset represents one reported crime and includes location, time, offense category, victim details, and, in many cases, weapon information.

Interaction Design

Dashboard 1 uses area and crime-type selections from our Part 2 notebook. Dashboard 2 uses victim-age ranges to drive the linked map and weapon chart.

Notebook Link

The data preparation and earlier expert-facing analysis are available in our Part 2 notebook, which this page adapts into a more public-facing format.

Dashboard 1

This dashboard is adapted from our Part 2 work. First click an LAPD area in the driver chart, then click a crime type if you want the monthly trend and heatmap to focus on one offense category inside that selected area.

Main View: Crime Incidents by LAPD Area

This driver plot shows total reported incidents by LAPD area and controls the other charts in Dashboard 1 and the contextual visualizations below.

Selecting a high-count area makes it easier to see whether the local crime profile differs from the citywide picture.

Driven Plot: Top Crime Types in Selected Area

This bar chart updates after area selection and also acts as a second filter for the timeline and heatmap.

Theft-related categories and vehicle-related offenses appear frequently, but the top ten ranking changes once one area is selected.

Driven Plot: Monthly Crime Trend

This line chart shows how incident counts change over time for the selected area and, when chosen, the selected crime type.

Driven Plot: Hour by Weekday Heatmap

This heatmap shows when incidents are most likely to occur based on hour of day and day of week.

Dashboard 1 provides an interactive overview of crime patterns in Los Angeles using the linked-view structure we developed in Part 2. The area chart works as the main entry point, the top crime-type chart narrows the focus further, and the monthly trend and heatmap reveal how timing changes once selections are made.

Dashboard 2

This second dashboard uses victim-age ranges as the interactive driver. The linked views show where incidents involving those age groups appear on the map and which weapon descriptions are most common for the selected range.

Main View: Victim Age Range Distribution

Click an age range in this area chart to filter the map and weapon bar chart.

Driven Plot: California Map with Incident Points

This map plots sample incident coordinates for the selected age range and shows latitude, longitude, and weapon description in the tooltip.

Driven Plot: Weapon Descriptions by Age Range

This chart highlights the most common weapon descriptions associated with the selected victim-age range.

Dashboard 2 extends the project beyond Part 2 by focusing on victim age. It shows how age groups differ in total involvement, where sampled incidents are located, and what weapon descriptions appear most often in those records. This helps connect demographic patterns to both geography and incident characteristics.

Contextual Visualization

These two supporting charts provide context beyond raw crime totals, which is one of the key rubric requirements.

Contextual View 1: Crime Burden Adjusted by Population

Incidents per 100,000 residents helps compare relative burden instead of only comparing total counts.

Contextual View 2: Poverty Rate and Crime Count

This bubble chart places area poverty rate beside reported incidents and uses bubble size to show estimated incidents per 100,000 residents.

Context data citations: Los Angeles City Planning: Community Plan Area data; U.S. Census ACS; Los Angeles Open Data Portal (Crime Data).

Conclusion

This project shows that crime in Los Angeles is not evenly distributed across space, category, or victim group. Some LAPD areas account for a noticeably larger share of incidents, and once one area is selected, the local crime composition and timing patterns can look different from the citywide view.

The second dashboard suggests that victim-age ranges also reveal meaningful differences. By connecting age bins to mapped incident locations and weapon descriptions, the analysis adds another layer to the public-facing story and helps viewers see how crime characteristics can vary across demographic groups.

The contextual charts strengthen interpretation beyond raw totals by showing that high counts do not always mean the highest relative burden. Important limitations remain: these data reflect reported incidents, later 2024 reporting may be less complete, and the contextual comparisons use area-level estimates rather than incident-level causes. Even with those limits, the dashboards provide a clearer view of crime patterns for a public audience.
